# NSF Grant Programs for CECREH

Analysis of NSF funding programs aligned with CECREH's mission of building capacity for resilient housing through AI, community engagement, and social science research.

---

## 1. Human-Centered Computing / Future CoRe (NSF 25-543)

**What it funds:** Research at the intersection of people and computing -- understanding how humans interact with, are affected by, and can be empowered by computing systems. Covers three scales: individual (HCI, accessibility), group (CSCW, collaboration), and societal (computing's broader community impacts).

**Program details:**

- **Award size:** Up to ~$1M over 4 years (typical $150--250K/year)
- **Duration:** Up to 4 years
- **Deadlines:** Rolling target dates in September and February annually
- **Requirements:** Standard NSF proposal; no community partner required
- **PI limit:** Max 2 proposals per PI across all Future CoRe programs per 12 months
- **Type:** Core/continuing program (always available)

**CECREH framing:**

Lead with HCI research questions, not the system. The AI Disaster Agent is the *instrument* for investigating fundamental questions about human-AI interaction, not the contribution itself. Emphasize:

- Trust calibration: How do citation strategies and authority-grounding affect user trust and decision quality during disaster scenarios, especially for populations with low digital literacy?
- Declarative governance as participatory design: Can a YAML-based skills framework enable emergency managers (non-technical domain experts) to meaningfully shape AI system behavior?
- Equity: Do AI disaster guidance systems reduce or amplify information access disparities across socioeconomic groups, renters vs. homeowners, and linguistically diverse communities?
- Cognitive load under crisis: How should conversational AI adapt its information presentation when users are under acute stress?

**Strengths:** Strong alignment with vulnerable populations, trust/transparency, and societal-scale computing. Gulf Hazard Interview data serves as preliminary results.

**Weaknesses:** Requires the heaviest reframing -- HCC funds research, not tool development. A proposal framed as "we built a system" will be desk-rejected. Need formal user studies. RAG + chatbot is now commodity; must foreground what is genuinely novel for HCI (governance framework, authority-grounded retrieval for safety-critical AI).

**Fit:** Good, with reframing

## 2. Responsible Design, Development, and Deployment of Technologies (NSF 25-536)

**What it funds:** Research on how to responsibly design, develop, and deploy technologies -- particularly AI -- with attention to societal impacts, governance, and ethical considerations. Explicitly includes disaster prevention and mitigation applications.

**Program details:**

- **Award size:** Phase 1 Planning: up to $300K/2yr; Phase 2: up to $1.5M/3yr
- **Duration:** 2--3 years depending on phase
- **Deadlines:** Check current solicitation for submission windows
- **Requirements:** Multi-sector team required (academia + industry/nonprofit/government)
- **Type:** Active program
- **Special:** Phase 1 is a planning grant -- low-risk entry point that builds toward Phase 2

**CECREH framing:**

This is the most natural NSF fit. Frame around responsible AI governance for safety-critical public systems:

- How should AI systems that provide life-safety guidance be governed, constrained, and made transparent?
- What design principles ensure authority-grounded information retrieval prevents misinformation amplification during crises?
- How can declarative governance frameworks enable non-technical stakeholders (emergency managers, housing authorities) to define AI behavior boundaries?
- What responsible deployment practices are needed for AI systems that serve vulnerable populations -- particularly renters -- during emergencies?

**Strengths:** Solicitation explicitly targets AI + disaster mitigation. Trust, transparency, and governance dimensions map directly. Multi-sector team requirement aligns naturally (Texas Tech + county EM office or FEMA region). Phase 1 planning grant is a low-risk entry point.

**Weaknesses:** Requires securing a non-academic partner for the multi-sector team.

**Fit:** Strongest NSF fit

## 3. Smart and Connected Communities (NSF 25-527)

**What it funds:** Research addressing community-identified challenges through integrative computing and engineering approaches deployed in partnership with community stakeholders. Emphasizes the bidirectional relationship between research and community needs.

**Program details:**

- **Award size:** SCC-DG (planning): $150K/1yr; SCC-IRG (full): up to $1.5M/3--4yr; SCC-LSR (large-scale): new category
- **Duration:** 1--4 years depending on track
- **Deadlines:** Two cycles per year (~Feb/Apr and ~Sep/Nov). Preliminary proposals required for IRG/LSR.
- **Requirements:** Community partner required (e.g., county emergency management office)
- **Type:** Continuing program
- **Special:** SCC-DG is a planning grant to develop the community partnership and pilot

**CECREH framing:**

Frame around community-deployed AI changing emergency preparedness behaviors and housing resilience outcomes:

- How does AI-mediated emergency guidance change community-level preparedness behaviors when deployed through local emergency management channels?
- What community-specific adaptations are needed when deploying AI disaster guidance across diverse Gulf Coast communities with different housing stocks?
- How should AI systems be co-designed with emergency managers and community members to address locally identified information gaps, particularly for renter populations?

**Strengths:** Very strong if a community partner is secured. SCC-DG planning grant is an excellent entry point to formalize a partnership. The community-deployed AI framing is natural. Can scale up to $1.5M through SCC-IRG.

**Weaknesses:** Requires an established or developing community partnership. Cannot submit without a community partner letter.

**Fit:** Very strong (requires community partner)

## 4. CHIRRP -- Confronting Hazards, Impacts, and Risks for a Resilient Planet

**What it funds:** Research to understand and reduce hazard-related risks through equitable community partnerships, earth system science approaches, and actionable solutions. Emphasizes convergence across natural science, social science, and engineering.

**Program details:**

- **Award size:** EAGER: up to $300K/2yr; RAISE: up to $1M/5yr
- **Duration:** 2--5 years
- **Deadlines:** Building capacity; check for current windows
- **Requirements:** Equitable community partnerships, actionable solutions
- **Type:** Newer program
- **Special:** EAGER grants have a streamlined review process

**CECREH framing:**

Frame around equitable housing resilience and community co-design:

- How do housing tenure patterns (renter vs. homeowner) shape vulnerability to and recovery from natural hazards along the Gulf Coast?
- Can AI-mediated emergency guidance reduce disparities in hazard information access across socioeconomic groups?
- How should hazard resilience technologies be co-designed with vulnerable communities to ensure equitable housing outcomes?

**Strengths:** Strong alignment with Gulf Hazard Interview work, renter/homeowner vulnerability research, and community equity focus. EAGER grants have streamlined review. CECREH's housing resilience mission maps directly.

**Weaknesses:** May require earth system science framing (integration with physical hazard modeling) that is not currently prominent in the project.

**Fit:** Strong

## 5. CIVIC Innovation Challenge (NSF 24-534)

**What it funds:** Community-driven, deployment-focused research addressing civic priorities. Two-stage competition: planning grants followed by full pilot awards. Track A focuses on climate resilience.

**Program details:**

- **Award size:** Stage 1: $75K/6mo (planning); Stage 2: $1M/12mo (pilot deployment)
- **Duration:** 6--18 months total
- **Deadlines:** Between rounds. CIVIC 3.0 Stage 2 awarded September 2025. Watch for CIVIC 4.0 announcement.
- **Requirements:** Community partner required. Short, deployment-focused timeline.
- **Type:** Periodic initiative (not always open)
- **Special:** Track A (climate resilience) is the relevant track

**CECREH framing:**

Frame around rapid pilot deployment with a community partner:

- Can an AI-powered emergency guidance system be successfully deployed through a county emergency management office to improve disaster preparedness in underserved communities?
- What adaptations are needed to move from prototype to community-deployed civic technology serving renter populations?

**Strengths:** The AI Disaster Agent is already at prototype stage -- ideal for CIVIC's deployment focus. Track A (climate resilience) is a natural fit. Fast timeline rewards projects with existing infrastructure.

**Weaknesses:** Not currently accepting applications. Short timeline demands a deployment-ready system and established community partnership.

**Fit:** Strong (when available)

## 6. RAPID Grants

**What it funds:** Time-sensitive research requiring urgent data collection in response to specific events (natural disasters, public health emergencies, etc.). Designed for perishable data that cannot be collected later.

**Program details:**

- **Award size:** Up to $200K
- **Duration:** Up to 2 years
- **Deadlines:** None -- submit anytime in response to a specific event
- **Requirements:** Must be tied to a specific disaster event. Short proposal (2--5 pages). Contact Program Director first.
- **Type:** Standing mechanism (always available)

**CECREH framing:**

Frame around collecting real-time data on housing vulnerability and information needs during an active disaster:

- What are the real-time information seeking patterns and housing-related needs of disaster-affected populations interacting with an AI guidance system during an active hurricane/flood event?
- How does AI-mediated emergency guidance usage differ between renters and homeowners during actual emergencies?

**Strengths:** No deadline -- submit when an event occurs. Short proposal. Can generate powerful preliminary data for larger proposals. The AI Disaster Agent is already designed for real-time deployment.

**Weaknesses:** Cannot plan around it -- requires a specific disaster event. Not a primary funding vehicle. Relatively small awards.

**Fit:** Complementary

## 7. Decision, Risk, and Management Sciences (DRMS)

**What it funds:** Research on judgment, decision-making, and risk perception. How people make decisions under uncertainty, how risk is perceived and communicated, and how decision processes can be improved.

**Program details:**

- **Award size:** ~$200--500K over 2--3 years
- **Duration:** 2--3 years
- **Deadlines:** Standing program with annual target dates
- **Requirements:** Standard NSF proposal
- **Type:** Core/continuing program

**CECREH framing:**

Frame around housing tenure and disaster risk decision-making:

- How do housing tenure (renter vs. homeowner) and socioeconomic factors shape disaster risk perception and protective action decision-making?
- What role does information source authority play in risk communication effectiveness during natural hazard events?

**Strengths:** Good fit for Gulf Hazard Interview analysis and renter/homeowner vulnerability research. No community partner required. Standing program with regular deadlines.

**Weaknesses:** Less natural fit for the AI system component. Smaller awards. Better suited to the social science research component alone.

**Fit:** Moderate

---

## 8. Strengthening American Infrastructure (NSF 25-534)

**What it funds:** Convergent research integrating social and behavioral sciences with infrastructure design, planning, and management.

**Program details:**

- **Award size:** Up to $750K over 3 years
- **Duration:** 3 years
- **Deadlines:** Through March 6, 2026
- **Requirements:** Convergent research across social/behavioral sciences and infrastructure disciplines
- **Type:** Active (check if continuing beyond current solicitation)

**CECREH framing:**

Frame around housing as critical infrastructure and emergency information systems:

- How should emergency information infrastructure be designed to account for the diverse decision-making processes and housing situations of disaster-affected populations?
- What role can AI-mediated information systems play as critical infrastructure supporting resilient housing outcomes?

**Strengths:** Good award size. Convergent research framing fits the combination of AI engineering and social science. CECREH's housing focus maps to infrastructure.

**Weaknesses:** Requires framing the AI system as "infrastructure," which is a stretch. Imminent deadline (March 2026). Moderate fit overall.

**Fit:** Moderate

**Note:** DRRG (Disaster Resilience Research Grants) has been sunset and is no longer accepting proposals.
