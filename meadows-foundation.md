# Meadows Foundation: Funding Guide for CECREH

Detailed analysis of the Meadows Foundation as a funding source for the Center of Excellence in Capacity-building for Resilient Housing (CECREH) at Texas Tech University.

---

## Foundation Overview

- **Founded:** 1948 by Algur H. and Virginia Meadows (General American Oil Company of Texas)
- **Mission:** "To assist the people and institutions of Texas improve the quality and circumstances of life for themselves and future generations"
- **Headquarters:** 3003 Swiss Avenue, Dallas, Texas 75204
- **Total assets (2024):** $718 million
- **Annual giving (2024):** $37.7 million in charitable disbursements; $24.1 million in direct grants across 160 awards
- **Total grants to date:** Over $1.4 billion to approximately 3,800 organizations across all 254 Texas counties
- **Geographic restriction:** Texas only -- all proposals must benefit the people of Texas

---

## Leadership (as of 2026)

- **Eric R. Meadows** -- President & CEO (effective January 1, 2026). Great-nephew of founders. Previously founded PM7 Construction, which specializes in **disaster recovery and hurricane restoration**. This background could signal increased institutional interest in disaster resilience.
- **Monica Christopher** -- Chief Philanthropy Officer (effective February 17, 2026). Previously Dallas Campus President of Texas Woman's University and SVP at Communities Foundation of Texas. Expertise in cross-sector partnerships and community impact.
- **Christina O'Brien** -- VP of Property & Climate Innovation. The creation of this role signals institutional commitment to climate-related work.

The new CEO's personal expertise in disaster recovery construction is notable for CECREH's proposal positioning.

## Grant Sizes

- **Typical range:** $10,000--$100,000 for standard grants
- **Full range (2024 data):** $500 to $4.9 million
- **Major investments:** Meadows Mental Health Policy Institute received $8.5M initial + two $10M five-year grants; Metro Dallas Homeless Alliance received $500K
- **Multi-year grants:** Approximately 50% of grants are single-year; multi-year grants are used for deeper strategic partnerships
- **Allocation by type:** ~85% goes to program/project support; capital support is secondary (green building preferred); general operating is a smaller share; endowments and scholarships are rarely funded

## Application Process

**No Letter of Intent required.** Submit a full proposal directly through the online portal.

**No deadlines.** Applications accepted year-round on a rolling basis.

**Timeline:** 4--5 months from submission to board decision. Apply at least 4 months before funding is needed.

**Board review schedule (5 meetings per year):**

| Board Meeting | Apply By (to be considered) |
| --- | --- |
| January | September of prior year |
| April | December of prior year |
| June | February |
| September | May |
| November | July |

**Process:**
1. Submit full proposal through the online grant portal (launched August 2025)
2. Proposal is assigned to a program officer who reviews in full
3. Program officer may request additional information or schedule a site visit
4. Program officer provides recommendation to the board
5. Board votes at the next scheduled meeting
6. Notification within 2 weeks of board decision

**Frequency limit:** One proposal per organization per 12-month period. Make it count.

**Staff support:** Program officers are available by phone (214-826-9431 or 800-826-9431) or email. Informational meetings can be scheduled before applying. They will not write your grant but will discuss fit and process.

## Eligibility

**Who can apply:**
- 501(c)(3) public charities
- Public entities (government agencies, public universities)
- Organizations need not be headquartered in Texas, but the program must directly benefit Texans

**Texas Tech University qualifies** as a public entity.

**Who cannot apply:**
- Individuals
- For-profit/commercial businesses

---

## What They Don't Fund

- Grants or loans to individuals
- Scholarships (rarely)
- Fundraising events or annual drives
- Professional conferences (unless directly tied to an initiative)
- Out-of-state travel
- Single artistic events
- For-profit businesses
- Endowments (rarely)
- Indirect costs are reviewed case-by-case; the foundation prefers "every dollar go toward direct services"

**Note on indirect costs:** This is important for university applicants. Texas Tech's standard F&A rate may not be accepted. Negotiate this early or budget with minimal/no indirect costs.

## CECREH Fit Assessment

### Best alignment: Human Services + Civic & Public Affairs

CECREH's strongest pitch to Meadows is through **Human Services** (crisis assistance for vulnerable populations) with a secondary framing under **Civic & Public Affairs** (capacity-building for community resilience).

### What resonates with Meadows

| CECREH Strength | Meadows Priority |
| --- | --- |
| Focus on renters and housing-vulnerable populations | Homelessness initiative; housing is a core concern |
| Gulf Coast disaster preparedness for underserved communities | Human Services: "compassionate assistance during crisis situations" |
| AI guidance system as civic infrastructure | Civic & Public Affairs: service delivery infrastructure |
| Texas-focused, Texas-serving | Geographic mandate -- they only fund Texas |
| Qualitative research with affected communities | Evidence-based, measurable outcomes |
| Capacity-building mission | Civic & Public Affairs: nonprofit capacity-building |

### What to emphasize

1. **People, not technology.** Meadows funds outcomes for Texans, not systems. Lead with who benefits: elderly residents who can't navigate emergency websites, renters who lack landlord communication during storms, families with medical equipment needs during power outages.

2. **Measurable outcomes.** The board looks for "quantitatively measurable outcomes with potential to scale impact." Propose specific metrics: number of residents reached, improvement in preparedness scores, reduction in calls to overwhelmed 911 systems.

3. **Leverage and catalytic role.** Show that Meadows funding unlocks larger grants. Position a $50K--$150K Meadows grant as enabling the community engagement work that makes NSF proposals ($300K--$1.5M) competitive.

4. **Texas scale.** The foundation has given across all 254 counties. Frame the AI Disaster Agent as scalable statewide, not just a Gulf Coast tool. Tornadoes, wildfires, and extreme heat affect all of Texas.

5. **New leadership alignment.** The new CEO's disaster recovery background and the VP of Property & Climate Innovation role suggest institutional momentum toward resilience work. This may be an opportune moment.

### What to avoid

- Don't lead with "AI," "RAG," "LLM," or technical architecture
- Don't frame this as basic research or technology development
- Don't request indirect costs at full university rates
- Don't propose work that primarily benefits researchers rather than communities
- Don't frame Texas Tech as the primary beneficiary -- the beneficiaries are Texas residents

### Recommended framing

> CECREH at Texas Tech University seeks to strengthen disaster preparedness for Texas's most vulnerable residents -- renters, elderly, and economically disadvantaged communities who face the greatest barriers to accessing accurate emergency information. Through qualitative research with Gulf Coast residents and a community-facing emergency guidance tool, this project addresses the information access gap that leaves housing-insecure Texans disproportionately harmed by hurricanes, floods, and extreme heat.

---

## Recommended Ask

### Option A: Community Engagement Pilot ($75K--$100K, 1 year)

Fund community engagement sessions with Gulf Coast renter populations and emergency managers to:
- Document information access barriers faced by housing-vulnerable Texans during disasters
- Co-design an emergency guidance tool with affected communities
- Pilot the tool with 2--3 community partner organizations
- Produce a replicable model for other Texas communities

This generates the community evidence base that strengthens NSF proposals (ReDDDoT, S&CC, HCC) and positions CECREH for larger Meadows investments.

### Option B: Gulf Coast Renter Vulnerability Study ($50K--$75K, 1 year)

Fund the packaging and dissemination of Gulf Hazard Interview findings to:
- Produce a policy brief on renter vulnerability in Texas coastal communities
- Present findings to TDEM, county EM offices, and housing authorities
- Convene a stakeholder workshop bringing together emergency managers and housing advocates
- Establish relationships with community partners for future funded work

Lower-risk ask that builds credibility and partnerships.

### Option C: Statewide Disaster Preparedness Initiative ($150K--$250K, 2 years)

Larger investment for demonstrated track record. Fund:
- Deployment of the emergency guidance tool in 3--5 Texas communities across hazard types (coastal hurricane, inland flood, wildfire, extreme heat)
- Community-based participatory research with vulnerable populations in each community
- Evaluation of impact on preparedness behaviors and information access equity
- Scalability assessment and statewide deployment roadmap

This positions CECREH as a statewide resource and aligns with Meadows's history of making larger investments in proven models.

## Application Strategy

1. **Call first.** Phone 214-826-9431 and ask to speak with a program officer in Human Services or Civic & Public Affairs. Describe the project and ask whether it aligns. This conversation will shape the proposal.

2. **Target the September board meeting.** Submit by May 2026 to be considered at the September meeting. This aligns with the Phase 0 foundation-building in the broader grant roadmap.

3. **Search their grants database.** Before applying, search [mfi.org/what-we-fund/search-previous-grants/](https://www.mfi.org/what-we-fund/search-previous-grants/) for comparable funded projects to calibrate expectations and framing.

4. **Show other funders.** Meadows wants to see their funding as catalytic. Mention pending or planned NSF proposals, GLO applications, and other funding sources in the proposal.

5. **Budget for direct services.** Minimize indirect costs and administrative overhead. Show that Meadows dollars go directly to community engagement, research participants, and tool deployment -- not university overhead.

6. **One shot per year.** Texas Tech can only submit one Meadows proposal per 12-month period. Coordinate across departments to ensure the strongest CECREH proposal isn't competing with other TTU requests.
